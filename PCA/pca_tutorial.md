# Principal Component Analysis — A Working Tutorial

PCA finds a lower-dimensional subspace onto which you can project your data while losing as little information as possible. This note develops it from two angles that turn out to be the same thing, works through the math carefully, and ends with the matrix/SVD view used in practice. Figures are generated by the Python script in the appendix.

---

## 1. Setup and notation

- Data: $n$ points $\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_n$, each in $\mathbb{R}^d$. Stack them as rows of a matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$.
- Mean: $\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_n \mathbf{x}_n$. **PCA always operates on centered data** — subtract $\bar{\mathbf{x}}$ from every point so the cloud is centered at the origin. After centering, "variance" and "average squared value" coincide, which simplifies everything below.
- Covariance matrix: $\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_n (\mathbf{x}_n-\bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_n-\bar{\mathbf{x}})^\mathrm{T} = \frac{1}{n}X_c^\mathrm{T}X_c$ (with $X_c$ the centered data). $\mathbf{S}$ is $d\times d$, symmetric, positive semi-definite.
- A projection direction is a **unit** vector $\mathbf{u}$ ($\mathbf{u}^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}=1$). The projection of a point onto it is the scalar $\mathbf{u}^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{x}_n$.

---

## 2. Two equivalent definitions

PCA can be defined two ways, and they give the identical answer:

1. **Maximum variance.** Find the subspace such that the projected points are as spread out as possible.
2. **Minimum reconstruction error.** Find the subspace such that the sum of squared distances from each point to its projection is as small as possible.

The equivalence is Pythagoras. For each point, (distance to origin)² splits into (projection length)² + (residual length)². The first term is what "variance" rewards; the second is the "error." Their sum is fixed per point, so making one big makes the other small.

![Projecting the data onto a 1-D subspace](fig1_projection.png)

*Left: projecting onto the principal direction — green projections are spread wide (high variance) and blue error segments are short. Right: projecting onto the orthogonal direction — projections collapse together (low variance) and errors are long. Same data, opposite extremes.*

Sweeping the projection line through all angles makes the trade-off explicit — the two curves are mirror images summing to the constant total variance:

## 3. Projected variance is a quadratic form

Take the first direction $\mathbf{u}_1$. The variance of the projected coordinates $\{\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{x}_n\}$ is

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\big\{\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{x}_n - \mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\bar{\mathbf{x}}\big\}^2
= \mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{S}\,\mathbf{u}_1 .
$$

**Derivation.** Factor $\mathbf{u}_1$ out of the bracket: $\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}(\mathbf{x}_n-\bar{\mathbf{x}})$. This is a *scalar*, call it $a$. A scalar equals its own transpose, so $a^2 = a\,a^\mathrm{T} = \mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}(\mathbf{x}_n-\bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_n-\bar{\mathbf{x}})^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_1$. The two vectors in the middle form a $d\times d$ outer product; $\mathbf{u}_1$ is pushed to both ends. Average over $n$ and pull the constant $\mathbf{u}_1$ outside the sum:

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum_n \mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}(\mathbf{x}_n-\bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_n-\bar{\mathbf{x}})^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_1
= \mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\Big[\underbrace{\tfrac{1}{n}\sum_n (\mathbf{x}_n-\bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_n-\bar{\mathbf{x}})^\mathrm{T}}_{=\;\mathbf{S}}\Big]\mathbf{u}_1 .
$$

The bracket is exactly $\mathbf{S}$. So **maximizing projected variance = maximizing $\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}_1$.**

---

## 4. The optimization: constraint, Lagrange, eigenvectors

### Why a constraint is needed

$\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}_1$ scales with $\|\mathbf{u}_1\|^2$: replacing $\mathbf{u}_1$ by $c\mathbf{u}_1$ multiplies the objective by $c^2$. Without a length limit the maximum is unbounded ($\|\mathbf{u}_1\|\to\infty$). But length is meaningless here — only the *direction* matters. So we fix the length with the normalization constraint

$$
\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_1 = 1 .
$$

### Lagrange multiplier

To maximize under an equality constraint, form the Lagrangian (the choice $1-\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_1$ over $\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_1-1$ is cosmetic — it only flips the sign of the multiplier, so the "$+$" convention keeps $\lambda$ positive):

$$
\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{u}_1,\lambda_1) = \mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}_1 + \lambda_1\big(1-\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_1\big).
$$

Setting $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\mathbf{u}_1 = 0$ gives $2\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}_1 - 2\lambda_1\mathbf{u}_1 = 0$, i.e. the **eigenvector equation**

$$
\boxed{\;\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}_1 = \lambda_1\mathbf{u}_1\;}
$$

Left-multiplying by $\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}$ and using $\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_1=1$ gives $\mathbf{u}_1^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}_1=\lambda_1$: **the variance captured equals the eigenvalue.** To maximize variance, pick the eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue. The next principal directions are the remaining eigenvectors in decreasing $\lambda$ order.

### Geometric reading of the Lagrange condition

Think of $f(\mathbf{u})=\mathbf{u}^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}$ as a landscape whose contours are ellipses, and the constraint $g(\mathbf{u})=\mathbf{u}^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}=1$ as a circular fence you must walk on. At the highest reachable point on the fence, the uphill direction $\nabla f$ has no component *along* the circle — otherwise you could step higher. So $\nabla f$ points straight *across* the circle, i.e. parallel to $\nabla g$. "Parallel gradients" is precisely $\nabla f = \lambda\nabla g$, and with $\nabla f = 2\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}$, $\nabla g = 2\mathbf{u}$ this is $\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}=\lambda\mathbf{u}$. Equivalently: the circle is *tangent* to a contour ellipse at the optimum.

![Lagrange geometry](fig3_lagrange.png)

*The pink unit circle is tangent to the elliptical contours of $u^TSu$ at the eigen-directions. At the max, $\nabla f$ (blue) and $\nabla g$ (gray) lie on the same line — that alignment is the equation $Su=\lambda u$. The minimum is the other eigenvector.*

## 5. Orthonormality and coordinates

The eigenvectors of the symmetric matrix $\mathbf{S}$ form an **orthonormal basis**:

$$
\mathbf{u}_i^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_j = \delta_{ij} \quad(1 \text{ if } i=j,\; 0 \text{ otherwise}).
$$

The $i=j$ case says each vector is unit length (normalization); the $i\ne j$ case says distinct vectors are perpendicular (automatic for a symmetric matrix's eigenvectors with distinct eigenvalues).

This gives a clean way to read off coordinates. Writing $\mathbf{x}=\sum_j a_j\mathbf{u}_j$ and dotting both sides with $\mathbf{u}_i$:

$$
\mathbf{u}_i^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{x} = \sum_j a_j\,(\mathbf{u}_i^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_j) = \sum_j a_j\,\delta_{ij} = a_i
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad a_i = \mathbf{u}_i^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{x}.
$$

Every coordinate is an **independent dot product** — no matrix inversion, because the $\delta_{ij}$ kills all cross-terms. This decoupling is what makes discarding directions safe: dropping some axes leaves the others untouched.

---

## 6. The reconstruction-error route

The second definition minimizes error directly. Approximate each point using $M<d$ directions, giving each point its own coordinate on the kept directions but a *shared* constant on the discarded ones:

$$
\widetilde{\mathbf{x}}_n = \sum_{i=1}^{M} z_{ni}\mathbf{u}_i + \sum_{i=M+1}^{D} b_i\mathbf{u}_i .
$$

The $z_{ni}$ carry each point's identity (they depend on $n$); the $b_i$ are one shared value per discarded axis (no $n$) — that's where the compression comes from. Minimize $J=\frac{1}{n}\sum_n\|\mathbf{x}_n-\widetilde{\mathbf{x}}_n\|^2$.

Because the basis is orthonormal, $\|\mathbf{x}_n-\widetilde{\mathbf{x}}_n\|^2$ collapses to a sum of squared coefficients (cross-terms vanish via $\delta_{ij}$):

$$
J = \frac{1}{n}\sum_n\Big[\sum_{i\le M}(\mathbf{x}_n^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_i - z_{ni})^2 + \sum_{i>M}(\mathbf{x}_n^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_i - b_i)^2\Big].
$$

Each unknown sits in its own squared term, so minimizing is immediate:

- $z_{ni}$ appears once → $\dfrac{\partial J}{\partial z_{ni}}=0 \Rightarrow \boxed{z_{ni}=\mathbf{x}_n^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_i}$ — the true projection (kept directions lose nothing).
- $b_i$ appears once per point → $\dfrac{\partial J}{\partial b_i}=0 \Rightarrow \boxed{b_i=\bar{\mathbf{x}}^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{u}_i}$ — the mean's coordinate (one value minimizing squared distance to all points is their average).

Substituting back, the kept-direction terms vanish and the leftover error is exactly the variance in the discarded directions:

$$
J = \sum_{i=M+1}^{D}\mathbf{u}_i^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}_i = \sum_{i=M+1}^{D}\lambda_i .
$$

Minimizing this means the *discarded* directions should be the smallest-eigenvalue eigenvectors — i.e. **keep the largest-eigenvalue directions.** Same answer as the variance route.

---

## 7. Matrix form and SVD

Collect everything into matrices. PCA factorizes the (centered) data:

$$
X \approx Z W, \qquad Z\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times k}, \quad W\in\mathbb{R}^{k\times d}.
$$

- **Rows of $W$** ($\mathbf{w}_j$) are the $k$ principal directions (the $\mathbf{u}_j$).
- **Rows of $Z$** ($\mathbf{z}_i$) are each point's $k$ coordinates (codes / features).
- Reconstruction of one point: $\mathbf{x}_i \approx \sum_{j=1}^{k} z_{ij}\mathbf{w}_j$.
- $k$ is the shared inner dimension — the compression knob.

**How you compute it:** center $X$; take the top-$k$ eigenvectors of $\mathbf{S}$ as the rows of $W$; set $Z = X_c W^\mathrm{T}$.

**SVD is the practical tool.** Any matrix factors as $X_c = U\Sigma V^\mathrm{T}$ with orthonormal $U,V$ and singular values $\sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\ge\cdots$ on $\Sigma$. Then:

- columns of $V$ = eigenvectors of $X_c^\mathrm{T}X_c$ = principal directions, so $W = V_k^\mathrm{T}$;
- $\sigma_i^2 = n\lambda_i$, so ordering by singular value orders by variance;
- codes $Z = U_k\Sigma_k$ (equivalently $X_c V_k$).

Multiplying back, $ZW = U_k\Sigma_k V_k^\mathrm{T}$ is the **truncated SVD**, which the Eckart–Young theorem proves is the *best possible* rank-$k$ approximation of $X_c$ in squared error — exactly the $J$ minimized by hand. In practice, running SVD on $X_c$ directly is more numerically stable than forming $\mathbf{S}=\frac{1}{n}X_c^\mathrm{T}X_c$ and eigendecomposing, which is why libraries (e.g. scikit-learn) do it that way.

---

## 8. Choosing $k$: variance explained

The variance captured by keeping $k$ components, as a fraction of the total:

$$
\text{variance explained} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k}\lambda_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{d}\lambda_i}
= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k}\sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{d}\sigma_i^2}
= 1 - \underbrace{\frac{\|ZW - X_c\|_F^2}{\|X_c\|_F^2}}_{\text{variance remaining}} .
$$

All three expressions are the same number in different languages:

| Quantity | Eigenvalue form | Frobenius form |
|---|---|---|
| Total variance | $\sum_{i=1}^d \lambda_i$ | $\|X_c\|_F^2$ (up to $\tfrac{1}{nd}$) |
| Variance remaining | $\sum_{i>k}\lambda_i \big/ \sum_i \lambda_i$ | $\|ZW-X_c\|_F^2 \big/ \|X_c\|_F^2$ |
| Variance explained | $\sum_{i\le k}\lambda_i \big/ \sum_i \lambda_i$ | $1 - \|ZW-X_c\|_F^2 \big/ \|X_c\|_F^2$ |

The bridge: $\|X_c\|_F^2=\sum_i\sigma_i^2$ (total), and $\|ZW-X_c\|_F^2=\sum_{i>k}\sigma_i^2$ (the discarded tail, by Eckart–Young). Variance remaining runs from $0$ at $k=d$ (exact) to $1$ at $k=0$ (nothing kept). The Frobenius form is handy because it needs only $X_c$ and its reconstruction — no eigenvalues.

A **scree plot** shows how variance is distributed and where the cumulative curve crosses a target (e.g. 90%):

![Scree plot and cumulative variance explained](fig4_scree.png)

*On the digits dataset ($d=64$), variance concentrates in the first few components; 90% is reached at $k=21$. The cumulative curve is the "variance explained" as a function of $k$.*

Because variance explained *is* reconstruction quality, growing $k$ visibly sharpens the reconstruction:

![Reconstruction at increasing k](fig5_reconstruction.png)

*One digit reconstructed from its top-$k$ components. A handful of directions already captures the gist; the rest add fine detail.*

> **Guaranteed floor.** Keeping $k$ of $d$ *ordered* components, pair each kept eigenvalue with a discarded one: $\lambda_1\ge\lambda_{d}$, etc. If you keep at least half the components, the kept sum dominates the discarded sum, so variance explained $\ge 50\%$ — regardless of the actual numbers. (This is why "keep 2 of 4 components" guarantees $\ge 50\%$.)

## 9. Two neighbors: VQ and the regression view

**Vector Quantization (VQ / k-means)** shares the exact $ZW$ skeleton but changes what the code $\mathbf{z}_i$ is allowed to be:

- **PCA:** $\mathbf{z}_i$ is a vector of real **weights**; reconstruct by *blending* all $k$ directions, $\widetilde{\mathbf{x}}_i=\sum_j z_{ij}\mathbf{w}_j$. A soft mix.
- **VQ:** $\mathbf{z}_i$ is a single **integer index** naming the nearest "mean" $\mathbf{w}_j$; reconstruct as $\widetilde{\mathbf{x}}_i=\mathbf{w}_{z_i}$. A hard assignment. Equivalently, VQ is PCA with $\mathbf{z}_i$ forced to be one-hot.

![PCA vs VQ](fig6_pca_vs_vq.png)

*Left: PCA ($k=1$) blends — every point slides onto the principal line via a real-valued weight. Right: VQ/k-means ($k=4$) snaps — every point jumps to its nearest centroid. Recipe vs label.*

**Regression view.** Reading $x_{ij}\approx\mathbf{z}_i^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{w}_{\bullet j}$ as a linear model: the shared code $\mathbf{z}_i$ is the *input*, feature value $x_{ij}$ is the *target*, and each **column** $\mathbf{w}_{\bullet j}$ of $W$ holds the regression coefficients predicting one feature from $\mathbf{z}_i$. PCA is $d$ linear regressions run in parallel — with the twist that the inputs $Z$ are not given but discovered jointly with the coefficients $W$ (which is what SVD solves in one shot).

## 10. The algorithm end-to-end

```python
import numpy as np

def pca_fit(X, k):
    """Fit PCA on training data X: (n, d). Returns everything needed to transform later."""
    mean = X.mean(axis=0)
    Xc = X - mean                                          # 1. center (store the mean!)
    U, sig, Vt = np.linalg.svd(Xc, full_matrices=False)   # 2. SVD
    W = Vt[:k]                                             # 3. top-k directions, (k, d)
    Z = Xc @ W.T                                           # 4. training codes, (n, k)
    evr = sig**2 / np.sum(sig**2)                          # explained variance ratio
    return {"mean": mean, "W": W, "Z": Z, "evr": evr}


def pca_transform(X_new, model):
    """Test phase: project NEW points onto the learned subspace. X_new: (m, d) -> (m, k).

    Uses the TRAINING mean and W — never recompute them on test data, or the
    new codes won't live in the same coordinate system as the training ones.
    """
    Xc_new = X_new - model["mean"]        # center with the TRAINING mean
    Z_new = Xc_new @ model["W"].T         # project: z = W x_c   (one dot product per direction)
    return Z_new


def pca_reconstruct(Z, model):
    """Map codes back to the original d-dim space. Z: (m, k) -> (m, d)."""
    return Z @ model["W"] + model["mean"]  # blend directions, then undo centering        
```

**Summary of the whole story.** Center the data. Maximizing projected variance and minimizing reconstruction error are the same objective (Pythagoras). Both reduce, via a Lagrange multiplier, to the eigenvector equation $\mathbf{S}\mathbf{u}=\lambda\mathbf{u}$: principal directions are eigenvectors of the covariance, ordered by eigenvalue = variance. Orthonormality makes coordinates independent dot products and makes the error split cleanly by direction. In matrix form PCA is $X\approx ZW$, computed as a truncated SVD, which is provably the optimal low-rank approximation. Variance explained — the fraction of spread you keep — tells you how many components to retain.
